Aqueous Solutions

An aqueous solution is a solution where water is used to dissolve some other substance.

  • A solution is a homogenous mixture of two or more substances
  • A solvent is the majority substance in a solution
  • A solute is the substance that is dissolved in the solvent
  • When baking soda gets dissolved in water, it creates and aqueous solution where water is the solvent, and baking soda is the solute

Electrolytes and Nonelectrolytes

Not all aqueous solutions are the same. Some are classified as electrolytes and others are classified as nonelectrolytes.

  • An electrolyte is a substance that has ions throughout it as an aqueous solution
  • A nonelectrolyte is a substance that lacks ions when it is an aqueous solution
  • Ionic compounds generally form electrolytes
  • When an ionic compound dissolves in a solvent it is generally said to disassociate into its component ions
  • Molecular compounds generally form nonelectrolytes

The Role of Water in Ionic Compounds

Water is a great solvent for ionic compounds because when the ionic compound begins to dissolve its component ions get swarmed with water molecules

  • When component ions are surrounded by water molecules it means that the ions have been solvated
  • If an ionic compound such as sodium chloride (NaCl) were to be solvated, it would be written as Na+(aq) and Cl-(aq) with the aq standing for aqueous
  • When an ionic compound gets solvated, cations and anions are less likely to recombine because the component ions have been stabilized by the water molecules
  • Ultimately, the ions get distributed through the solution in a uniform manner
