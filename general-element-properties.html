Matter and its Phases or States

Matter is the material that makes up the universe, and it consists of tiny particles called atoms, ions, and molecules, can exist in solid, liquid, or gas phases or states. These particles are always moving, and increased temperature makes them move faster while a decrease in temperature makes them move slower. The weight of these particles also influeces their speed.

Solids are a phase of matter with definite volume and shape.

  • They are composed of tightly-bound particles that are held together by very strong attractive forces.
  • These particles vibrate around one another.
  • When heated, solids slightly increase in size through a process known as expansion.
  • When cooled, solids slightly decrease in size through a process known as contraction.

Liquids are a phase of matter with indefinite shape and definite volume.

  • The particles in liquids are very close together, but they can roll around each other, which allows liquids to take the shape of their containers.
  • The forces of attraction in liquids are weaker than they are in solids, but liquid particles usually have more energy than solid particles.
  • Temperature has a slight influence on the volume of liquids.
  • Volume can be slightly reduced with pressure through the process of compression.

Gases are a phase of matter with indefinite shape and indefinate volume.

  • Gases will take the shape of their container AND spread out evenly through it.
  • The volume of gases is heavily influenced by temperature.
  • Particles in gases are far apart and move around randomly at a high velocity.
  • There are very weak forces of attraction in gases.
  • Volume can be substantially reduced with pressure through the process of compression.

Diffusion is the process through which gases spread out.

  • Different gases and liquids diffuse at different rates
  • Diffusion can also occur in liquids, but it is much slower
  • Intimate mixing is the diffusion between liquids and gases

Changes in Phase: Melting and Boiling Points

A substance's phase changes based on the melting and boiling point of that substance. For example, if a solid substance has a melting poing of 100 °C, it will turn into a liquid if heated to that degree. If the substance has a boiling point of 120 °C, it will start turning into a gas at that degree.

When heat is added phases change from solid -> liquid -> gas. When the substance is cooled, phases change in the order of gas -> liquid -> solid.

  • Changes in state or phase are physical changes, which means that no new substances are formed
  • Melting and boiling points can be used to figure out the identity of unknown substances since one can take note of the temperature they melt and boil at and reference it against the known elements
  • When a solid has reached its melting point its particles gain the ability to move around each other instead of just vibrating together
  • A solid will not reach a higher temperature than its melting point until it has been melted
  • When a liquid has reached its boiling point gas bubbles will start to form inside the liquid and rise up while the liquid evaporates
  • Solids melt into liquids and liquids boil into gases
  • Gases condense into liquids and liquids freeze into solids

Sublimation is the rapid spreading out of particles that results in a solid changing to a gas while skipping over the liquid phase.

  • In such instances, when the gas cools it immediately turns into a solid, once again skipping the liquid phase

Atoms

Atoms are the smallest particles of elements that maintain their identity during a physical or chemical change, and they are made of electrons, protons, and neutrons. They have a very dense, positively charged nucleus that is surrounded by electrons.

  • Electrons have a negative charge and are often symbolized with e-
  • Neutrons have no charge and are often represented as n but you might also see n0
  • Protons have a positive charge and are often symbolized with p or p+
  • Protons and neutrons are similar sizes, but electrons are much smaller than them.
  • Atomic number, or Z, shows the number of protons in an atom's nucleus
  • If an atom is neutral. it contains the same amount of electrons outside the nucleus as protons in the nucleus

Molecules and Compounds

A molecule is two or more atoms that are bonded together (in units called particles) in consistent ratios.

  • Molecules have neutral charges
  • Polyatomic molecules are molecules that have two or more atoms

Diatomic molecules are molecules that are made up of two of the same atom. Here are the naturally occuring diatomic elements on Earth:

  • Bromine
  • Chlorine
  • Flourine
  • Hydrogen
  • Iodine
  • Nitrogen
  • Oxygen

Ions

An ion is an atom that has an electric charge.

  • Cations are ions that are positively charged
  • Anions are ions that are negatively charged
  • An ion's charge is usually included as a superscript on ionic chemical symbols when writing their formulas OH-1
